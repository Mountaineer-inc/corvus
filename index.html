What we are building

Corvus is not a holding company in the traditional sense. It is an outdoor industry platform — designed from the ground up to own the infrastructure, the IP, the brands, and the technology that the next generation of the hunting, fishing, and tactical markets will be built on.

Every company in this portfolio is connected. Every piece of IP lives inside Corvus. Every brand, every technology, every licensing relationship — owned, controlled, and compounding in value inside a single structure. The sum is considerably larger than the parts.

Capital partner preferred return — Don & Dusty Bishop
What this is and why it matters

Don and Dusty Bishop are putting capital at risk with no salary and no operational compensation. Their entire return depends on the portfolio growing and eventually distributing or exiting. A preferred return gives them guaranteed current yield on their invested capital — paid before any discretionary distributions happen — so they're not waiting years for any financial return on money they've already committed.

This is standard in capital partner arrangements. It's not a salary — it's a return on invested capital, similar to interest on a loan but structured as a distribution priority. It protects the capital partners without affecting the operating dynamics of the business.

Preferred return mechanics
The rate

6–8% annually on total invested capital. If Don and Dusty Bishop combined invest $500K, the preferred return generates $30–40K per year in priority distributions. The exact rate is negotiated and documented in the operating agreement before execution.

When it pays

Activates when Corvus has distributable cash above the mandatory tax distribution threshold. Not paid from operating cash needed for the business. Priority is: operating expenses → tax distributions → preferred return → discretionary distributions.

Cumulative or non-cumulative

Should be cumulative — if a year produces no distributable cash and the preferred return isn't paid, it accrues and must be paid before any future discretionary distributions. This protects capital partners in lean years without straining the business in the short term.

At exit

Any accrued but unpaid preferred return is settled before exit proceeds distribute to equity holders. Capital partners receive their original capital back plus any accrued preferred return, then remaining proceeds distribute by ownership percentage.

Cash distribution waterfall — the priority sequence

Every dollar Corvus generates moves through this sequence in order. Nothing skips the line. This is written into the operating agreement and is not subject to discretionary decisions at any step except the final one.

1
Operating expenses including salaries

Joseph's salary, Drew's salary, Kevin's salary (at Tarn level), benefits, overhead, software, professional services. The business runs before anyone gets paid a distribution.

2
Mandatory tax distributions

Quarterly distributions to all members sufficient to cover their estimated tax liability on allocated income. Non-discretionary — required by the operating agreement regardless of other decisions. Protects everyone from phantom income tax bills.

3
Preferred return to capital partners

6–8% annually on Don and Dusty Bishop's invested capital, paid as a priority distribution before discretionary distributions run. Cumulative — accrues if not paid and must be settled before any discretionary distribution goes out. This is the capital partners' guaranteed current yield.

4
Capital contribution repayment (if applicable)

If the operating agreement includes a capital return provision — repayment of original invested capital before equity distributions — it happens here. This is optional and negotiated. Many structures skip this step and go directly to equity distributions.

5
Discretionary distributions by equity percentage

Everything remaining distributes to Don Bishop, Dusty Bishop, and Joseph per their ownership percentages. This is the only step that requires a decision — how much to distribute vs. retain for reinvestment. All steps above are mandatory and happen first regardless of this decision.

The S-Corp preferred return mechanism requires CPA confirmation. In an S-Corp structure all distributions must be pro-rata per share — a separate class of preferred distribution could violate the single class of stock rule and trigger loss of S-Corp status. The CPA needs to confirm the right instrument — either a distribution priority provision that functions as a preferred return, or a revaluation of whether the S-Corp election is the right structure given the capital partner payment requirements. This is a critical item for the professional team before any documents are executed.

For the attorney and CPA — technical detail on preferred returns in S-Corps

The single class of stock problem. IRC §1361(b)(1)(D) prohibits S-Corps from having more than one class of stock. Treasury Regulation §1.1361-1(l) defines a second class as existing when ownership interests confer different distribution or liquidation rights. A preferred return that gives capital partners priority distributions creates a facially different economic right from ordinary distributions — potentially triggering a second class determination and involuntary S-Corp termination with retroactive C-Corp tax treatment.

The safe harbor — identical liquidation and distribution rights. The same-class rule requires that all outstanding shares have identical rights to distribution and liquidation proceeds. However, differences in timing or amounts of distributions do not violate this rule if they result from legitimate business decisions — not from the governing documents themselves creating different rights. A distribution priority provision in the operating agreement that functions as a preferred return may or may not violate this rule depending on how it's drafted. This requires attorney review.

Clean alternatives that avoid the problem. (1) Structure Don and Dusty Bishop's preferred return as a promissory note or guaranteed payment separate from distributions — this is debt, not equity, and avoids the class issue entirely. (2) Revisit whether the S-Corp election is right for this structure — if the portfolio is heading toward multiple equity classes, preferred returns, and complex waterfall provisions, an LLC taxed as a partnership may be the more appropriate vehicle despite the self-employment tax cost. (3) Issue a different type of instrument — warrants, options, or convertible notes — to capital partners in addition to equity, providing current yield through debt service rather than distribution priority.

The recommendation for counsel. Before executing the operating agreement, have the CPA model the annual self-employment tax cost of abandoning the S-Corp election vs. the tax cost of involuntary S-Corp termination if a preferred return triggers a second-class finding. The right answer depends on projected income levels, the capital partners' marginal rates, and the specific waterfall provisions contemplated. Do not draft preferred return language without this analysis in hand.

Personnel equity pool
The simple idea

We set aside a slice of Corvus before anyone's ownership percentage is finalized. That slice — the equity pool — is reserved specifically to reward the people who help build this thing over time. When someone earns their way into it, they get real economic participation in the upside. No voting rights, no board seats, just a meaningful financial stake that grows in value as the portfolio grows. It's the carrot that attracts and keeps great people without giving away control.

The two paths — pick one

There is a structural conflict between two things we want: an S-Corp tax election and a true equity pool. We can have both, but not in the same exact form. Below is a plain-language explanation of each path. The recommendation follows.

Path 1 — Phantom Equity Pool
Works with S-Corp · simpler to set up · less tax-efficient for recipients
How it works in plain language

Phantom equity isn't real ownership — it's a contractual promise that says "when this company sells or distributes profits, you get a percentage as if you owned X%." The person receives cash at the right moments, but they never appear on the ownership documents. It's a bonus tied to company value.

The tax reality

When a phantom equity holder gets paid — at exit or through profit distributions — that money is taxed as ordinary income. In 2026 that means up to 37% federal plus NC's 4.5%. A $200,000 phantom equity payout nets roughly $116,000 after taxes. That's real money but it's the less efficient path.

Why it works with S-Corp

Phantom equity holders aren't actually members of the LLC. They have a contract, not an ownership stake. That means the S-Corp election stays clean — one class of ownership, all distributions pro-rata among actual members. The phantom pool sits outside the ownership structure entirely.

Works well becauseSimple to administer. No new ownership documents when someone earns in. Doesn't touch the S-Corp structure. Can be structured with flexible payout triggers.
The downsideRecipients pay the highest tax rate on their payout. Over time this is meaningfully less valuable to the people we're trying to retain than real equity would be.
Path 2 — Profits Interest Pool
Requires LLC partnership taxation · more setup · significantly better for recipients
How it works in plain language

A profits interest is real ownership in the LLC, but granted at zero current value. The recipient only participates in growth that happens after their grant date — they don't share in what was already built. It's a way to give someone a genuine stake in the future without diluting the founding team's existing value.

The tax reality

When a profits interest holder gets paid at exit, it's taxed as long-term capital gains — currently 20% federal maximum plus NC's 4.5%. That same $200,000 payout nets roughly $151,000 after taxes. Compared to the phantom equity path, the recipient keeps $35,000 more on the same dollar amount. That difference compounds significantly on larger exits.

The structural requirement

This only works if Corvus is taxed as a partnership — meaning we do not elect S-Corp status. We can still structure reasonable salaries and distributions thoughtfully, but we lose the specific self-employment tax savings the S-Corp election provides. The CPA needs to run the numbers on whether the tax savings for profits interest recipients outweighs the self-employment tax cost to Joseph and Drew.

Works well becauseRecipients pay capital gains rates, not ordinary income rates. Real ownership creates stronger alignment and retention. Cleaner and more meaningful for key performers.
The downsideRequires giving up the S-Corp election. More complex to set up and administer. Each grant requires a valuation or safe harbor determination. New members appear on ownership documents.

Our recommendation — Path 1 now, Path 2 later
Start with phantom equity. Revisit when the portfolio is profitable enough to model the real numbers.

Here's the honest rationale. Right now Corvus is pre-revenue. The S-Corp election saves Joseph and Drew real money on self-employment taxes once salaries are active — that benefit is near-term and certain. The profits interest advantage only matters when there's a meaningful exit, which is 3-5 years away. Starting with phantom equity preserves the S-Corp savings now and keeps the structure simple while the portfolio is being built.

As the portfolio matures and the CPA can model actual exit scenarios, the decision to convert or restructure can be made with real numbers. A restructuring from S-Corp to partnership taxation is not trivial but it's also not unusual — companies do it when the equity compensation argument becomes compelling enough.

What should not wait: defining the pool size, the vesting terms, the payout triggers, and the performance conditions. Those go in the operating agreement now regardless of which path is chosen. The instrument can evolve. The framework shouldn't.

How the pool works in practice
Pool size & current allocation

10% of total Corvus ownership reserved before founding splits are finalized. The founding three each contribute proportionally — their percentages are calculated on the remaining 90%. Drew Mangus receives the first grant: 4% vesting over 4 years with a 1-year cliff. This leaves 6% in reserve for future key hires — operations managers at the subsidiary level, future executives, and anyone else who earns their way in over time. Ungranted pool shares have no economic effect until issued.

Who controls grants

Joseph as CEO controls pool grants for standard performers, with notification to Don and Dusty Bishop. Grants above a defined threshold — say 2% or more — require majority partner approval. No one can grant themselves additional equity from the pool.

Vesting

Standard vesting: 4-year total, 1-year cliff. Nothing is earned in year one. After year one, 25% vests immediately, then monthly over the remaining 3 years. Departure before the cliff means the grant is forfeited entirely. This protects the company from granting equity to someone who doesn't stay.

No voting rights

Pool recipients have economic participation only. They receive their share of distributions and exit proceeds. They do not vote on company decisions, do not sit on any governance body, and are not members of Corvus in the governance sense. This is a financial instrument, not a control instrument.

Payout triggers

Recipients receive their pro-rata share when Corvus makes a distribution to founding members, and at exit when a subsidiary sells. Phantom equity holders receive cash payments matching what their percentage would have earned. The mandatory tax distribution clause (see Tax Protection tab) covers them too.

Departure terms

Vested grants survive departure — the person keeps what they earned. Unvested grants are forfeited. The operating agreement defines whether a departing member's vested phantom equity pays out immediately, deferred to the next distribution, or at exit only. This needs to be decided upfront and documented precisely.

For the attorney and CPA — technical detail

S-Corp single class of stock rule (IRC §1361(b)(1)(D)). S-Corps are prohibited from having more than one class of stock. Treasury Regulations define a second class as existing when ownership interests confer different distribution or liquidation rights. Phantom equity avoids this because recipients hold a contractual right, not a membership interest. Profits interests create actual membership interests with different economic rights from founding members, which risks violating the single class rule and terminating the S-Corp election retroactively. This is the core reason Path 1 is recommended while S-Corp status is in place.

Phantom equity taxation. Payouts are ordinary income to recipients under IRC §83 and subject to FICA if the recipient is also an employee. Structure payout triggers carefully — distributions tied to exit proceeds may be treated differently than annual profit distributions. The plan document must specify whether payouts are tied to cash distributions, EBITDA targets, or exit events only.

Profits interest safe harbor (Rev. Proc. 93-27, Rev. Proc. 2001-43). A profits interest granted in exchange for services is not taxable at grant if it meets the IRS safe harbor: granted at fair market value, not a substantially certain right to a fixed income stream, not publicly traded. Each grant requires a contemporaneous valuation or Section 83(b) election documentation. The LLC must be taxed as a partnership for this to apply.

North Carolina conformity. NC generally conforms to federal partnership and S-Corp treatment. NC individual income tax rate is currently 4.5% (continuing to phase down). The combined marginal rate assumption for tax distribution calculations should be confirmed with the CPA at time of operating agreement drafting — the rate is moving.

Pool reserve mechanics. The operating agreement should specify: authorized pool size as a percentage of fully-diluted membership interests; grant authority and approval thresholds; standard form of grant agreement; vesting schedule defaults and permissible variations; treatment of unvested grants on change of control; and whether ungranted pool interests are dilutive to existing members or only dilute upon grant. These mechanics need attorney drafting — they cannot be left to interpretation.

Tax protection for all partners
The problem we're solving

When a company makes money, the IRS expects every owner to pay taxes on their share — even if the company kept the cash and never paid it out. This is called phantom income. It's a real tax bill on money you never received. If the company holds cash for reinvestment and doesn't distribute enough to cover each partner's tax bill, partners get hit with large tax payments out of their own pockets. This has driven people out of ownership in otherwise healthy companies. We are building a mandatory protection against this from day one.

The solution — mandatory tax distributions
How it works in plain language

The operating agreement will require Corvus to distribute enough cash to each partner every quarter to cover their estimated tax bill on their share of company income. This happens automatically — it is not a vote, not a discussion, not subject to Joseph's discretion or anyone else's. It is a contractual obligation of the company. The company must pay it before any other decision about what to do with cash.

Everyone covered: Don Bishop, Dusty Bishop, Joseph, Drew, and any equity pool recipients who have vested interests. Nobody pays taxes on money they didn't receive.

How the number is calculated
1
Determine each partner's share of taxable income

At the end of each quarter, Corvus calculates its net taxable income for the period. Each partner's allocated share is their ownership percentage times that income. A partner with 33.3% ownership who receives a K-1 showing $600,000 of company income owes taxes on $200,000 — whether or not they received a dollar of it.

2
Apply the assumed tax rate

The operating agreement specifies a single assumed combined tax rate — federal plus North Carolina state — that is used for all partners. We use the highest rate applicable to any member so everyone is fully covered. Based on current rates: 37% federal + 4.5% NC = approximately 41.5% combined. The CPA confirms and updates this rate when tax law changes.

3
Distribute that amount to each partner

Corvus distributes cash to each partner equal to their allocated income multiplied by the assumed rate. On $200,000 of allocated income at 41.5%, that partner receives $83,000. They now have the cash to pay their tax bill. If they're in a lower bracket, they keep the difference. If they're in a higher bracket, the distribution covers most of it.

4
Additional distributions are a separate decision

The tax distribution is a floor, not a ceiling. After it's made, the partners can decide whether to distribute additional profits or retain them for reinvestment. That decision is governed by the distribution policy in the operating agreement. What's non-negotiable is the tax distribution. Everything above it is discretionary.

Distribution policy — above and beyond the tax distribution
How we handle profit above the tax distribution

Once the mandatory tax distributions are made, what happens to remaining profit is a policy decision that should be defined in the operating agreement — not left to case-by-case votes that create tension. A reasonable framework:

Early stage (Year 1–2)
Retain all profit above tax distributions for reinvestment. The portfolio needs capital to grow. No discretionary distributions until the portfolio is self-sustaining.
Growth stage (Year 3+)
Distribute a defined percentage of annual profit above the tax distribution — suggested starting point is 25-33% distributed, remainder retained. Reviewed annually by partners.
Exit events
Full proceeds distributed promptly after exit costs, debt obligations, and any defined reserves are satisfied. No discretion — automatic.
For the attorney and CPA — technical detail

Phantom income in pass-through entities. Under Subchapter S and partnership taxation, each member's share of entity income is allocated on the K-1 regardless of whether cash was distributed. Members pay tax on their K-1 allocation at their individual rates. The mandatory tax distribution clause addresses this by ensuring the entity distributes sufficient cash to cover each member's estimated liability before retaining earnings.

Assumed tax rate considerations. The operating agreement should specify the assumed rate as the highest combined marginal rate applicable to any member — federal ordinary income (currently 37%), net investment income tax if applicable (3.8% on passive income above thresholds), and NC individual rate (currently 4.5%, phasing down). For passive investors like Don and Dusty Bishop, long-term capital gains rates (20% + 3.8% NIIT + NC 4.5%) apply to exit proceeds but ordinary rates apply to operating income allocations. The distribution formula should use ordinary income rates to ensure full coverage on operating income, with a true-up mechanism at exit when capital gains rates apply.

Quarterly vs. annual distributions. Quarterly distributions tied to estimated tax payment deadlines (April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15) reduce the cash flow mismatch. Annual distributions create a situation where partners may have already paid estimated taxes out of pocket before the distribution is made. Quarterly is strongly preferred operationally.

Interaction with S-Corp reasonable compensation rules. Under S-Corp rules, Joseph and Drew must receive reasonable compensation (W2 salary) before distributions. Reasonable compensation reduces the entity's pass-through income, which reduces the tax distribution obligation. The CPA should model the interaction between salary levels and tax distribution amounts annually.

Phantom equity holder tax distributions. Phantom equity holders are not members and do not receive K-1 allocations. Their payouts are treated as compensation or bonus income when made, not as pass-through income. The tax distribution clause as written applies to members only. Phantom equity plan documents should address whether the company will gross up payments to cover the tax cost — a separate but related consideration.

Operating agreement drafting requirements. The mandatory tax distribution clause must specify: calculation methodology and assumed rate; timing of distributions (quarterly recommended); treatment of losses in prior periods that offset current income; priority of tax distributions relative to other claims on cash; whether the clause can be suspended and under what conditions (recommend: only by unanimous consent); and the mechanism for true-up if actual tax liability differs from estimated distributions. This clause requires attorney drafting and CPA input on the rate and calculation methodology — it cannot be templated.
